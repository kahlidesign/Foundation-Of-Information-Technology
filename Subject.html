FOUNDATION OF INFORMATION TECHNOLOGY

Objective 3: Data, Information & Knowledge

What is Data?

Data refers to raw, unprocessed facts, figures, or statistics. It lacks context and organization, making it difficult to interpret on its own. It can be:

  • Qualitative (subjective, opinion-based): e.g., survey responses about customer satisfaction.
  • Quantitative (objective, measurable): e.g., temperature readings over time.
  • Discrete (separate, unrelated pieces): e.g., a list of student names.
  • Detailed or Sampled (precise vs. representative): e.g., a full database of financial transactions vs. a sample of selected transactions.

What is Information?

Information is processed and structured data that provides meaning and can be used for decision-making. It may involve:

  • Distortion or Disguise - Selective presentation to alter perception.
  • Reliability & Consistency - How dependable the information is.
  • Interpretation & Comprehensibility - How users perceive it.
  • Value & Relevance - Whether it is useful for a given purpose.
  • Confidentiality & Security - Protection measures.
  • Timeliness & Completeness - How current and comprehensive it is.
  • Shareability & Availability - Ease of access and distribution.
  • Format & Medium - Whether presented as text, images, graphs, reports, etc.
  • Lifespan - Short-term (news reports) or long-term (historical records).
  • Information as a Commodity - Bought, sold, and used for business or research.

An example: A spreadsheet of customer transactions becomes information when analyzed to show purchasing trends over months.

What is Knowledge?

Knowledge is derived from analyzing and applying information. It involves understanding relationships, contexts, and implications, leading to decision-making. Important aspects include:

  • Use of Information - Applying insights effectively.
  • Data Quality - Ensuring accuracy and relevance.
  • Appropriateness - Selecting the right data for a given problem.
